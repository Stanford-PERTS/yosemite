Persistence and the Common Core

The Common Core standards push students to work on longer and more challenging problems—ones that require high degrees of persistence. This means that students may encounter more setbacks and more struggle than they are accustomed to.

Her insights and experience will guide us through many parts of this course.

The importance of a growth mindset for persistence

As we discussed, students will have to be far more persistent to succeed under the Common Core. A large body of research demonstrates that students with a growth mindset are better placed to succeed when learning is tough. What is a growth mindset?


Video Summary

  • Students with a growth mindset believe that their abilities grow with effort and experience. These students learn from their mistakes, and they persist on challenging tasks.
  • Students with a fixed mindset believe that abilities are a gift that a person either has or doesn't have. These students hate to fail (it makes them feel dumb) and so they avoid challenging work.
  • Different mindsets have important implications for learning

Instilling a growth mindset

In a seminal study, Professor Carol Dweck and her colleagues taught middle school math students a growth mindset through a workshop about the brain. These students became more motivated and earned higher grades than students in a control group who just learned study skills. We'll be hearing more from Carol Dweck later in this course.

High school students and college students show the same basic pattern. So there is now compelling evidence that learning how intelligence can grow can help all people learn in more adaptive ways, especially when material is challenging as in the Common Core.

Background on learning and neuroplasticity

Intelligence is about connections

What does research about the brain show? Can people really increase their intelligence?

Recent neuroscience findings show that the brain is much more capable of change than we previously thought. The outside layer of the brain, called the cortex, is made up of billions of nerve cells called neurons. When people learn new things, the connections between their neurons multiply and strengthen. When individuals engage in effortful practice, stronger connections get made and things that once seemed hard or impossible, like doing calculus or writing well, become easier. Over time, making many such connections results in a stronger, smarter brain.

Click on the video to learn more about neuroplasticity.


Video Summary

  • Brains have a lifelong capacity to change and rewire.
  • Connections between neurons can always be built and strengthened.

The video presents remarkable examples of neuroplasticity, such as recovering functions after removing half of the brain or learning the street system of one of the world's most complex cities. Neuroplasticity can occur across all students, in all classrooms. Contrary to the idea that people are born smart, average, or dumb and stay that way for life, research shows that all of us (from babies to adults) can get stronger and smarter as we learn and exercise our brains.

Environments shape brain growth

Scientists noticed striking differences in the brains of rats who were raised in stimulating and challenging environments compared to those who were raised in sparse and isolated environments.

In a challenging environment, the animals were constantly active, exercising their brains by exploring toys and developing relationships with other animals. But animals who were isolated spent their time just eating and sleeping. The active rats grew more neuronal connections. Compared to the inactive rats, the active rats became better at solving problems and learning new things.

The same applies in the classroom. Cultivating a challenging atmosphere in a classroom encourages students to build skills that allow their brains to grow.

Every student comes into the classroom with different skills based on their past learning. Some students may have never encountered geometry, while others may have spent the summer practicing geometry problems. It can be hard to convince students that they can improve their abilities, especially for students who notice that others are learning more quickly than they are. These students can benefit from being reminded that everyone comes into the classroom with different past experiences. Students may start out in different places, but they can all grow their abilities when their environment:

  • provides them with challenges that exercise their brain
  • reflects the growth mindset they need to embrace challenges

Mindset Messages

How can we build a classroom environment that fosters a growth mindset? A first step is to be aware of the messages we send to students.

This means we need to carefully examine our own beliefs and mindsets. Everyone has fixed mindset thoughts sometimes, even people with a growth mindset. For example, a person who has a growth mindset about history might have a fixed mindset about math. Or an educator might believe that a student is talented in art but can't do math. Or that some students can get smarter, but others cannot. It is important to remember that teachers can transmit these messages to their students.

Stanford Psychology Professor Carol Dweck explains how we can start understanding and responding to our fixed mindset thoughts.


Video Summary

  • Mindset messages can be explicit in the words you use. But they can also be communicated implicitly in the activities and lessons you design.
  • We should be aware of our thoughts and change our practices to foster a growth mindset in class.

Make Struggle a Good Thing in Your Class

Students look up to their teachers. One way you can help your students develop a growth mindset is by sharing a story of when it took you time to master something. If you once struggled with math and yet are now teaching math, how did you get better at it? Recall how you felt during the struggle; think about what useful strategies you practiced to learn math, and share these with your students.

Praise the Process: Effort, Strategies, Progress

Another way you can promote a growth mindset is through how you praise. When a child takes on a hard problem and makes progress, compliment their effort, their strategies, or their progress. Do not praise children's abilities or tell them "You're smart"it puts them in a fixed mindset, and makes them afraid of challenge.

Mindset Messages: Encouraging Students

Here's another way to help your students develop a growth mindset. Before a student attempts a new problem, you can give them growth mindset encouragement. Tell them how the problem can help them get smarter in math or grow their math brain.


Video Summary

  • Students who received simple growth mindset messages before math problems solved more problems than students who received no message or a standard encouragement message ("Try your best.").
  • Improvements continued on future problems even when the growth mindset message was no longer presented.


PERTS study with Khan Academy

Instilling a growth mindset takes time

It can be challenging to help students develop a growth mindset. Students will not adopt a growth mindset right away; it is a process. Try guiding students towards it little by little. For example, if a student perceives just one hard problem as an opportunity to grow, that's a good start.

Put another way, have a growth mindset about instilling a growth mindset! It will surely take time and effort, but it gets easier, and it pays off with more motivated and more interested students.

Activity 2: Helping your students adopt a growth mindset

We've discussed how to establish a growth mindset culture. The goal of this activity is to take these ideas to teach one lesson that exemplifies a growth mindset for your students.

There are many ways to change students' views about their ability to learn. We encourage you to try something new. Be creative and explore ways to help students see obstacles as opportunities to grow their abilities. Here are some starter ideas from teachers we have worked with in the past.

  • Have students write about something that was hard for them at first but that they eventually learned. Share your own example. Lead a class discussion about how you can grow your brain when you try hard, keep going, and choose challenging problems.
  • Explain that it's through struggle, mistakes, and trying new strategies that people learn and their brains grows. Give a personal example!

Warning! Think about your students' perspective.

Sometimes well-intentioned plans to convey a growth mindset can undermine the core message. Do you think the two statements below would help develop a growth mindset in students?

  • Whenever students get something wrong, I'll tell them, “Try harder and you'll get it”.
  • Teach them about growth mindset and fixed mindset, and tell them they should have a growth mindset.

The first asks students to put in more effort, but it does not help them understand how to do it or why effort itself is important. In fact, many kids believe that if you're good at something you shouldn't need effort. They believe that effort is evidence that they're not smart. Instead, students need to understand that trying hard on a difficult problem is what grows their brain.

The second statement falls prey to a common trap: telling adolescents what to do is a sure way to get them to do something else. Simply telling students to adopt a growth mindset because it's good for them is likely to meet resistance. We must explain to students the neurological basis for believing in a growth mindset and create an environment that cultivates these beliefs.

Mistakes in math

In this session, we will focus on the importance of mistakes for students' success in school and beyond. The new Common Core standards encourage students to challenge themselves because research shows that students learn more deeply when they have the opportunity to struggle, to make mistakes, and to learn from their mistakes. Challenging schoolwork is critical for students' intellectual development. It pushes students to expand their abilities and gives them the opportunity to practice perseverance and critical thinking—skills that will ultimately help them succeed in school and beyond.

Yet, as many teachers are well aware, students often shy away from challenging schoolwork or get quickly discouraged by it. In this session, we review research on the importance of challenge and struggle for learning, and we discuss the role that students' mindsets play in how they respond to mistakes and difficulties. We then focus on what teachers can do.

Mistakes in math

Learning a new math concept for the first time is like exploring a strange new island. An explorer might wander around and sometimes have to backtrack. That's how they learn the geography. In the same way, students sometimes need to explore when they learn math. They try things out. Some work. Some don't. Each time, a thoughtful student can ask themselves, "Why did this work? Why didn't that work?" They can start to make sense of it through trial and error. But they can only do so if they are comfortable thinking about their mistakes and learning from them.

This willingness to make mistakes and to learn from them is not just important in math class. It is important to success in a number of life domains. In the video below, Professor Boaler talks about modern students' fears of mistakes.


Video Summary

  • Successful entrepreneurs need mistakes.
  • We are raising a generation of students who are afraid of mistakes. We need to help this generation value and learn from mistakes.

Promoting challenges and mistakes

When teachers challenge students with difficult assignments, they give them the opportunity to learn more in the present, and they help them develop the capacity to learn more in the future. In the present, challenging problems push students to explore concepts deeply and to make the types of mistakes that help them learn. In the long run, challenging work—when scaffolded the right way—helps students become comfortable with mistakes and see them as a natural part of the learning process. Professor Jo Boaler explains the importance of challenging problems and the value of making and learning from mistakes:

Why should teachers assign challenging coursework?


Video Summary

  • Students learn most when they make mistakes—provided that they have a mindset that lets them learn from mistakes instead of ignoring them.
  • Students don't learn when they get all of their work correct.
  • Encourage students to choose problems that challenge them.
  • Teach methods by introducing a new type of problem before explaining the solution: let students struggle with a new concept.

Responding to mistakes

Students learn the most when they do hard work, make mistakes, and learn from these mistakes. Unfortunately, many students feel crushed when they make a mistake. They think it means they are not a math person or aren't smart. Does that mean teachers should avoid assigning challenging work? No! It means that teachers should create a learning environment in which students feel good about making mistakes—because they know it's how they learn—and value this opportunity.

Recent research has found that the brains of individuals who think they can learn from their mistakes respond differently than the brains of individuals who think intelligence is fixed. Professor Carol Dweck explains one such study:


Video Summary

  • Students with a fixed mindset ignore their mistakes.
  • Students with a growth mindset focus on their mistakes and process them deeply so they can learn from them.

Make challenge the new comfort zone

When we asked teachers to change their lesson plans to ensure that students have the opportunity to make and learn from mistakes, some were initially hesitant to do so. This is because teachers see their job as helping students and making sure they succeed. We understand that it seems counterintuitive to be pleased when students make mistakes, but these are the most important learning times for students. As Professor Carol Dweck describes in the video below, effortless success should not be celebrated. Instead, we must help students become comfortable being challenged.


Video Summary

  • Getting lots of easy problems right should not be a cause for celebration.
  • "Easy" means that you're not learning as much as you could.
  • Make challenge the new comfort zone.
  • We want students to ask for more challenging work.

We all want our students to feel good in class, but that does not mean we should assign easy work. If students never get accustomed to making mistakes and learning from them, they will avoid hard problems in the future. This type of mistake avoidance has been found to promote cheating and to hinder learning.

In contrast, students are liberated when they learn to view mistakes with a growth mindset. In a growth mindset, students are comfortable being challenged because they don't feel stupid when they (inevitably) make mistakes. Rather, they view mistakes as an important part of the learning process.

How to put it into practice

It is one thing to believe in the power of mistakes, but how do we get our students to pay attention to their mistakes and learn from them? Simply giving students harder assignments does not encourage learning. We need to teach students how mistakes help us learn.

Professor Jo Boaler introduces us to some of the strategies to help students start valuing mistakes:


Video Summary

  • Create the norm that you love and want mistakes.
  • Don't just praise mistakes - explain why they are important.
  • Give work that encourages mistakes.


Correct answers can help you learn too

Of course, correct answers can be valuable for learning as well—especially if students make a point to learn from them. For example, when students answer a problem correctly, a teacher can push them to think more deeply by asking, "Why is this right? Why isn't that right?" The key is active learning.

A culture of mistakes and challenges

When teachers create a more challenging classroom structure, students may be uncomfortable at first. One way to make students more comfortable with mistakes and challenges is to emphasize the learning process over scores or speed.

Some teachers track students' progress and use statements such as, “Not quite there yet,” “Getting better,” and “Your hard work paid off!” to communicate the importance of the learning process to their students. Remind students that the work is tough on purpose—so that they have the opportunity to grow their abilities—and make it clear that mistakes are tools for learning.

Video Summary

  • Show this progress to students frequently.
  • Tell students: “You have learned challenging subjects before, and you can learn this.”
  • Use feedback to teach students how to become better in the future.

A culture of mistakes and challenges

Even when we attempt to change the classroom culture about mistakes, some students may still feel discouraged by challenges and frustrated about making mistakes. As Carol Dweck describes, teachers have tried a number of practices to help these students.

Activity 3: Keep those mistakes

On the next assignment, ask students not to erase anything, but instead to put any mistakes they make in square brackets. Tell them that when you see their work, you want to see their whole process, including their mistakes along the way. Activity 4: Crumpled reminder

Give each student a piece of paper, ask them to crumple it up and throw it at the board with the feelings they have when they make a mistake in math. Get them to retrieve the paper, uncrumple it, and color each line with different colors. Tell your students that these lines represent all the synaptic activity that happens when a mistake is made. Explain how they can learn from mistakes. Ask them to keep the paper and stick it into a notebook or folder to look at when they make a mistake.

This physical reminder prompts students to use mistakes to strengthen their brain every time they open their notebook.

Growth mindset tasks

Challenging tasks provide opportunities for students to struggle and understand content more deeply.

Students given 20 short answer math questions typically think their role is to get them right—to perform, rather than to learn. When tasks are opened up to give more space for learning inside them, then students are more likely to see math as about learning.


Video Summary

  • Small changes in tasks can reinforce “learning,” not just “performing.”
  • Asking students to make choices in tasks spurs a learning process.

An implicit contract

Giving challenging tasks, as you may have discovered yourself, is easier said than done. As Professor Boaler explains, an implicit contract exists between teachers and students that limits how much students are willing to struggle through problems.


Video Summary

The didactic contract:

  • Students want to be led through the material step by step.
  • Being led removes the challenge from the student.
  • Teachers are naturally inclined to help.
  • Giving immediate help removes cognitive demand.

Question: Do you recognize this contract? What might you do to change this contract and ensure that students stay engaged with hard problems?

Help students welcome struggle

There are dozens of ways teachers can help students welcome struggle as an opportunity to learn. Below are a few of our favorites:

If a student asks for step-by-step help, guide the student through the challenge by

  • Asking them to explain the problem to you, and what step might be missing.
  • Suggesting they draw a visual representation of the problem.
  • Asking them to present what they don't understand to the class, as others are challenged by the same thing.
  • Explaining that it's great they are being challenged and allow them to struggle with the concept for a few minutes before giving them a hint.
  • Asking them what are some possible steps forward and discussing how to work through this challenge together without leading them directly

Now that we recognize the didactic contract, we can both challenge our students and guide them in the right direction to keep them engaged with hard problems. On the next pages we'll dive into how to create those challenging tasks that lead to growth and deeper learning.

The importance of collaboration

Especially in math classes, it can be difficult to ensure deep understanding because students can learn a rule without understanding why that rule makes sense.

As you will see in the video on the next page, one way to enhance learning is to ask students to explain and convince the class of their answer. This requires students to think through the problem more deeplyand it can also help the rest of the class see the problem from a different perspective. In this way, teachers can create a culture of working together to make sense of problems.

In the video below, Professor Jo Boaler explains the culture of collaboration that Cathy Humphreys, a middle school teacher, has created in her classroom. Cathy has co-authored a book with Jo Boaler on Mathematical lesson plans, and is an expert on getting students to "make sense" of mathematical concepts.


Video Summary

  • Encourage students to make sense of a problem.
    • When an answer is right, ask why is this right? Why isn't another answer right?
  • Create a culture of collaboration.
  • View mistakes as something valuable and to be shared.

Encouraging deeper learning

Once we encourage students to make sense of problems, we can enhance deeper learning.

Typically, students are asked to answer math questions that have one right answer, rather than questions that foster a deep understanding of a concept. In a growth mindset task, such as that shown in the video below, students can be asked to find patterns and to gain a conceptual understanding prior to a mathemetical understanding.

Here, Professor Boaler gives one example of a growth mindset task that asks students to visually understand a concept that is difficult at first.


Video Summary

  • Multiple correct answers encourage a growth mindset.

Assessment for Learning

Professor Boaler says that we live in a testing culture, which focuses on constant evaluation and speed. This is why so many students feel anxious in math give up rather than persevere. She explains:


Video Summary

  • Constant testing focuses on right or wrong. That makes students afraid of making mistakes and discourages them from diving into material more deeply.
  • Timed tests convey that getting answers quickly is more important than fully understanding concepts.

Feedback to promote a growth mindset

In most classrooms, students take a test at the end of a unit to see if they have learned the material or not. However, assessments are critical during learning—to shed light on students' learning processes, what they have learned, and what they haven't learned yet.

Depending on your school structure, some of our suggestions may be easier or harder to implement, but you can use the basic ideas to create new types of assessments for your students.

Here, Professor Boaler describes the importance of feedback in instilling a growth mindset:


Video Summary

  • How teachers give feedback influences students' mindsets.
  • Right vs. wrong feedback makes it seem like school is about performing, not learning. It doesn't encourage students to growth and improvement.
  • Summative assessments = end of unit assessments showing performance.
  • Formative assessments = assessment that informs learning for both student and teacher—assessment for learning.

Assessment for Learning

Assessments for learning are “check-ins” or markers of a student's learning progress. The feedback is primarily descriptive. It emphasizes strengths, identifies challenges, and points to next steps for the student. As teachers track students' understanding of course material, they can adjust their instruction to make sure students keep progressing along their learning paths.

Professor Jo Boaler discusses recent research that supports an assessment-for-learning approach:


Video Summary

  • Formative feedback could raise U.S. academic rankings.
  • Students should have a full and clear sense of their learning path.
    1. Where they are in the course.
    2. Where they need to be.
    3. How to close the gap between a and b.
  • Students who receive diagnostic feedback outperform students who receive grades.
  • Diagnostic feedback without a grade attached helps students focus on how to improve.

Formative assessments can take quite a bit of time and effort. Sometimes it is difficult to provide this feedback on a frequent basis. So self and peer assessments can be a great way for students to assess themselves. Professor Boaler provides a quick example of a self-assessment:

Activity 1: Introduce participation "quizzes"

In a participation quiz, students work together in small groups on a particular topic. Each group receives a grade for its work as a whole.

This is a wonderful way to increase students' engagement in group work. It turns the focus from getting problems right to learning as a group.

Before you start:

  • Give students a clear idea of what they should be doing mathematically—what problem or concept they are to work on. Here is an example of one teachers learning goals for students.
  • Give students a clear idea of how you want them to work in groups. Discuss the criteria for good participation (for which you will hold students accountable), such as:
    • Equal participation.
    • Students should think out loud, share ideas, and give each other feedback.
    • Everyone in the group should be able to explain the concept, idea, or answer.

Here is an example of one teachers guidelines for group work.

Students then work in small groups. As they work, walk around and briefly note positive or negative interactions. You can write these down on a private piece of paper, or record them on a board for all students to see. At the end of the lesson give each group a grade. Here is an example of one teachers notes and grades.

Before trying a participation quiz some teachers worry that it may be hard to keep track of how so many groups are doing, but teachers generally find it manageable, even with 8 or 9 groups. Teachers' notes are typically very brief and help them assign a grade to each group for how well it worked.

Some teachers find that students' group work does not change much in the first quiz but changes greatly after the first or second quiz. Once students receive a grade for how well their group worked together they take group work more seriously. The next time they work harder to make group work equal, that is, to encourage everyone to speak, to look for shared understanding, to help peers who don't yet understand the material, etc. Some teachers find that participation quizzes completely change group work in their classes.

Remember to make sure students know exactly what you expect in each group. Hold students accountable for working well togethermake sure they participate equally and that each student can explain the concept, idea, or answer to each other or to you. You can hold students accountable by asking one student in each group to explain the concept or answer a question. If the student cannot answer, move on to the next group, as it is the group's responsibility that everyone understand. Tell the student to listen to students in other groups or to work with their own group to learn. Later on, return to groups where students could not answer the question and ask the same student to share what they have learned.

Activity 2: Assessment for Learning

Here are two assessments for learning: self-assessments and peer assessments. Try one or both!

1. Self-assessments:

In self-assessments, students evaluate their learning against specific learning goals. Set learning goals for your students in your current unit. Write the learning goals on the board for all to see.

On a regular basis (e.g., twice a week), ask students to evaluate how well they are progressing toward each learning goal. For instance, students could assess their progress toward the following learning goals:

  • I understand how to calculate the mean of a set of numbers.
  • I understand the difference between mode and median.
  • I can give an example of when mode, mean, and median would be used.

Students assess themselves against these learning goals on a 3-point scale For example:

  • 1—I don't understand this yet.
  • 2—I have some understanding of this.
  • 3—I fully understand this.

Self-assessments are meant to help both you and the student understand how students are progressing toward learning goals. It is important for students to see their progress. They also serve the purpose of helping students understand what they are meant to be learning, which is an awareness that many students lack and that is key to high achievement.

As described below, have your students share their self-assessments with you so you can modify your teaching to best support your students learning.

2. Peer assessments:

Like self-assessments, peer assessments rely on specific criteria to assess students learning and highlight the important concepts students should be thinking about. Students give feedback on each others work based on these criteria. For example, students can assess a peers homework or in-class work and give it one or more stars and wishes. Stars represent what the student has done well. Wishes represent how the student could improve. Have students share their peer assessments with each other and with you.

Use your students' assessments:

  • to adjust your teaching pace or focus as necessary.
  • to identify and help individual students who have not yet understood a particular concept or idea.
  • to challenge individual students with new questions or work who have understood a particular concept or idea.

Assessment for learning should be a back-and-forth process: You get feedback from your students about their understanding, which helps you adjust your teaching. Students think about their own understanding, which they share with teachers. They learn:

  • Where they are in their learning.
  • Where they should be.
  • How they can close the gap between the 2 places.

When teachers and students engage in assessment for learning, you can teach more effectively and students learn to take more responsibility for their learning as they become more aware of what they need to learn. Assessment for learning has a substantial research base that shows that it leads to large achievement gains.

It might take a few times for students to get comfortable with self and peer assessments and to use them effectively, so try it 3-5 times.

Activity 3: Use inverted tests to teach students a growth mindset

Tell students that homework and tests are for learning—that when you get your work back you should look for mistakes you made so you can grow and learn from them.

As practice, give students a test completed by a fictitious student who didn't do very well. Make sure this fictitious student showed his or her work on the problems. Ask your students to correct this fictitious student's test. Students should:

  • Identify what the fictitious student understood well.
  • Identify mistakes the fictitious student made.
  • Explain each mistake, the conceptual idea relevant to each mistake, and how they themselves would approach or solve the problem.

Evaluate students in terms of how many mistakes they identify and how well they explain the mistakes and underlying conceptual ideas.

Use this exercise instead of a traditional test it truly taps students' understanding. Try it 2-3 times to help students get the hang of it.

Remind students that when they get their own tests and homework back they should do the same thing—look for and learn from mistakes. You can even assign students to do this activity using their own work.

Program Summary

We hope you enjoyed the program. Below is a summary of what we covered. You can also download this pdf to remind yourself of the key points of this program.

Session 1:

Although neuroscience shows that the brain can change and grow, many students believe that intelligence is a fixed trait. When students hold this "fixed mindset," they give up easily in the face of challenges because they think that effort, mistakes, or difficulty mean theyre not smart. This is troubling for educators, especially with the introduction of the Common Core. The new standards call on educators to challenge students to promote deeper learning. Students who have a growth mindset, who believe that intelligence can grow, view challenge as an opportunity to learn. As educators, we can prepare our students to excel in challenging learning environments by fostering a growth mindset.

Session 2:

One way to foster a growth mindset is to help students understand that challenge and mistakes are opportunities to learn and grow their brains. Research shows that students learn the most when they make mistakes and struggle on hard problems. Educators can encourage productive struggle and mistakes by assigning new types of problems, celebrating mistakes rather than perfect scores, explaining to students why mistakes are valuable, and helping students recognize the progress they have made.

Session 3:

Another way to foster a growth mindset and encourage productive struggle is to assign richer, more open-ended tasks. Typical math problems with one right answer can discourage a growth mindset. Educators can modify tasks in their classrooms by allowing students to come up with problems on their own, encouraging productive struggle instead of quickly telling students the answer, focusing on "sense-making" rather than following set rules to be memorized, and creating a culture of collaboration and problem-solving among students.

Session 4:

Teachers can also encourage a growth mindset by changing how they assess students. Typical assessment practices can inadvertently convey fixed mindset ideas . For example, timed tests can suggest that getting answers quickly is better than fully understanding concepts. Educators can modify tasks by giving formative feedback, using assessments to provide students with a clear sense of their learning paths, shifting the focus from performance to understanding areas for improvement, setting learning goals for students, utilizing self and peer assessment to encourage students to take responsibility for their own learning, and focusing on the work students have done (the process) rather than their ability.
