      Professional Development

      The Growth Mindset for Math online professional development program was created in collaboration with Jo Boaler, Carol Dweck, and middle and high school math teachers. It gives educators practical advice on how to promote adaptive mindsets in their students through specific classroom practices. Designed for middle and high school math teachers, these online modules include summaries of the latest research, videos, and downloadable actives. This program will be open for anyone to use.

      Topics include:

      • Introduction to growth mindset
      • The importance of mistakes and productive struggle in math
      • Growth mindset tasks
      • Assessment for learning

      Available January 2015

       

      Readings

      Learn about the science behind our program. Below are links to papers related to academic mindset research.

      Learn About Mindsets

      CCSR Report: Teaching Adolescents To Become Learners—The Role of Noncognitive Factors in Shaping School Performance: A Critical Literature Review

      This report provides an excellent and comprehensive overview of the research on non-cognitive factors—including mindsets—related to student success. Each factor is linked to the academic behaviors that flow from them which provides a clear framework for understanding why non-cognitive factors are such strong predictors of student success.

      Farrington, C. A., Roderick, M., Allensworth, E., Nagaoka, J., Keyes, T. S., Johnson, D. W., & Beechum, N. O. (2012). Teaching adolescents to become learners: The role of noncognitive factors in shaping school performance. Chicago, IL: 154 University of Chicago, Consortium on Chicago School Research.

      Parent Praise to 1- to 3-Year-Olds Predicts Children’s Motivational Frameworks 5 Years Later

      Laboratory studies have shown that praising children’s effort can lead them to adopt a growth mindset. This study examined the impact of parent’s naturalistic praise on their children’s mindsets. Different kinds of praise are described including interesting findings about the prevalence of each. The impact of parent’s praise of children’s effort at 14-38 months was linked to children having a growth mindset at 7-8 years of age.

      Gunderson, E. A., Gripshover, S. J., Romero, C., Dweck, C. S., Goldin-Meadow, S., & Levine, S. C. (2013). Parent praise to 1-3 year-olds predicts children’s motivational frameworks 5 years later. Child Development. 84(5), 1526-1541.

      Learn About Mindset Interventions

      Social-Psychological Interventions in Education: They’re Not Magic

      Written for educators, this paper reviews the theoretical basis of several prominent social-psychological interventions including those that target implicit theories (mindsets about intelligence, for example), belonging uncertainty and stereo-type threat. It provides a clear explanation of how it‘s possible for brief interventions to have lasting effects; that their power comes from targeting students’ subjective experiences in school, using persuasive yet stealthy methods for conveying psychological ideas, and tapping into recursive processes present in educational environments.

      Yeager, D. S., & Walton, G. M. (2011). Social-psychological interventions in education: They’re not magic. Review of Educational Research, 81(2), 267–301.

      The New Science of Wise Psychology

      This paper provides an excellent summary of how and why social- psychology interventions work. It includes a well organized overview of 18 intervention studies conducted in a wide variety of contexts, most of which were with students.

      Walton, G. M. (2014). The new science of wise psychological interventions. Current Directions in Psychological Science. 23(1) 73-82.

      Implicit Theories of Intelligence Predict Achievement Across an Adolescent Transition: A Longitudinal Study and an Intervention

      Two studies that were foundational to mindset research are presented in this paper. In the first study, 373 students entering 7th grade were surveyed for fixed or growth mindset beliefs then tracked for 2 years. Students with a growth mindset did significantly better academically. In the second study, an intervention to teach a growth mindset successfully improved achievement of students with a fixed mindset.

      Blackwell, L. S., Trzesniewski, K. H., & Dweck, C. S. (2007). Implicit theories of intelligence predict achievement across an adolescent transition: A longitudinal study and an intervention. Child Development, 78(1), 246–263.

      Academic and Emotional Functioning in Middle School: The Role of Implicit Theories

      In this study, 115 students entering middle school were surveyed on their beliefs about the malleability of intelligence and whether they believed emotions could be controlled. Students beliefs about intelligence predicted their academic outcomes and students beliefs about their ability to control their emotions predicted well-being. This study was foundational to the development of our Middle School Transition program which teaches students that challenging emotions can be managed and provides them with strategies to do so.

      Romero, C., Master, A., Paunesku, D., Dweck, C. S., & Gross, J. J. (2014, February 10). Academic and Emotional Functioning in Middle School: The Role of Implicit Theories. Emotion. Advance online publication. http://dx.doi.org/10.1037/a0035490

      Learn About Scaling Mindset Interventions

      How Can We Instill Productive Mindsets at Scale? A Review of the Evidence and an Initial R&D Agenda

      This 2013 white paper was prepared for the White House convening on Excellence in Education: The Importance of Academic Mindsets. It reviews research on academic mindsets and programs, including several PERTS program, that have demonstrated effective methods for scaling interventions targeting mindsets. It concludes with a discussion of issues still to be explored and recommendations for next steps for testing successful interventions at scaling.

      White House meeting on Excellence in Education: The Importance of Academic Mindsets. (2013). How Can We Instill Productive Mindsets at Scale? A Review of the Evidence and an Initial R&D Agenda [White Paper]. Stanford, California.
